Content Culture

The Scramble to Seem Human Is Missing the Point

What happened:

  • After years of AI content flooding social feeds and inboxes, people are pushing back. Brands and creators are rethinking everything.
  • Early 2026, major brands including Equinox and Almond Breeze ran ads mocking AI content. They wanted to look human and real.
  • Influencer agencies are getting requests for content that looks messy on purpose. An unmade bed, dishes in the sink, a shirt that hasn't been ironed.
  • Consumer preference for AI creator content has dropped from 60% in 2023 to 26% today.
  • Instagram CEO Adam Mosseri released a memo in December asking platforms to label AI content and show people who's really posting.

Diane's Take

In 2008, Zhang Yimou directed the opening ceremony of the Beijing Olympics. Thousands of performers moved in perfect sync. The world watched and thought ... China has a lot of people.

Yimou saw something else. He saw an abundant resource and asked what he could build with it. He stopped seeing it as a problem and started seeing it as a canvas.

AI has done the same thing to content. Now anyone can write a clean paragraph. Anyone can fill a newsletter. A well-written email, a polished case study, a professional website ... none of that proves anything anymore. It is just the starting line.

So brands are panicking. They ask creators to leave the dishes in the sink. They run ads mocking their own industry. They scramble to prove they're human.

But let's name what's underneath all of that. Doesn't the hunger for lower staff costs, so owners and companies keep more profit and more power, sound familiar? It sounds like the Gold Rush. Everyone racing to stake a claim before someone else does. Whoever gets there first controls the ground, the water, and every tool everyone else has to rent.

Are we in a race where one powerful group ends up holding all the keys?

And then there's a whole new problem on top of that. AI detectors have flooded the market. Clients and employers are drawing hard lines. No AI content allowed. We have serious detectors and you will be deleted and blocked. So people pour money into AI detector subscriptions. You run your content through three of them. You pass on one. You score 70% on the next. The third one calls it AI-created.

That is not the right question to be asking.

The real question is whether what you write is actually true. Whether it came from a real moment. Whether you were there.

Real means you were there. You made the decision. You learned the lesson. You carried the failure. You remember the exact moment things changed.

AI can write about resilience. It cannot endure. AI can write about failure. It cannot fail. AI can describe the moment a client called to say thank you. It was not on that call.

The organizations that earn real trust stop racing to label their content human. They fill it with things only they could know.

26%

That is the share of consumers who now prefer AI-generated creator content, down from 60% in 2023, according to research from influencer marketing agency Billion Dollar Boy, cited in Digiday. In three years, people lost their taste for it. They went looking for something real. They found it.

The Question Nobody Wants to Ask

If AI saves us all that time, where does it go?

AI is here to save us time. To make us more efficient. To give us all the data we need to solve real problems for real businesses. That is the promise. And for a lot of companies, it is working.

But here's what I keep turning over in my head ... efficient for whom?

Because when you walk out your front door, you see people. Real ones. Some are doing okay. A lot of them are not. And no AI was built to care about that gap.

There is a scene in Slumdog Millionaire where the camera pulls back and you see the whole city. The kids on the corners. The landfill at the edge of everything. The movie does not fix it. It just asks you to look.

Soylent Green was supposed to be science fiction. Will I end up walking in that line?

When a tool is only measured by what it produces, clicks and content and numbers on a dashboard, the older question gets lost. The harder one. How do we love our neighbor? How do we care for the poor, the underserved, the animals that need care, the living creatures who never show up in the efficiency metrics because they are not in the market at all?

That question gets louder as the tools get smarter.

I write for organizations that are still trying to answer it. Nonprofits, clinics, trade businesses, local organizations doing work that a machine can help with but never replace. I write for them because the work matters. Because someone has to say out loud what an organization stands for and why it is worth staying.

That still has to be a human job. And I am grateful every time someone trusts me with it.

One Small Thing

When your client can't give you a story, write your own first.

Ghostwriting has a quiet problem. Clients are busy. They hand off the intake form to an assistant. They assume you can fill in the gaps. They trust you to sound like them before they have shown you what that sounds like.

Here is what I keep coming back to. I spent years in sales making a hundred calls a day while a boss timed and recorded every one. Those calls taught me what it means to be accountable to a moment, to another person, to the truth of what actually happened. That experience lives in me. And when I write for someone else, I am looking for the same thing in them.

The practice that keeps helping me is writing my own story first. The rough version. The one where I was nervous, or wrong, or just figuring it out. When you know what it costs to write something true from your own life, you recognize the same material in someone else. You know which question to ask. And you know how to wait for the real answer.

Try it this week. Write one true moment from your own experience. Just the moment, not the lesson. Notice how long it takes to get there. That is what you are asking of every client who trusts you with their story.

Something Found

From Charles Duhigg, Supercommunicators

Every conversation is really one of three conversations. Do you want to be helped? Do you want to be hugged? Or do you want to be heard?

It stopped me because it is so simple it should be obvious. Somehow it is not. I think about how many client calls would go differently if I asked that first. Before the intake form. Before the questions. Just ... which conversation do we actually need to have right now?
